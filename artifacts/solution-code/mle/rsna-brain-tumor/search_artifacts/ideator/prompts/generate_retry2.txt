# Problem
# RSNA-MICCAI Brain Tumor Radiogenomic Classification

## Description

A malignant tumor in the brain is a life-threatening condition. Known as glioblastoma, it's both the most common form of brain cancer in adults and the one with the worst prognosis, with median survival being less than a year. The presence of a specific genetic sequence in the tumor known as MGMT promoter methylation has been shown to be a favorable prognostic factor and a strong predictor of responsiveness to chemotherapy.

![](https://storage.googleapis.com/kaggle-media/competitions/RSNA-2021/image2.png)

Currently, genetic analysis of cancer requires surgery to extract a tissue sample. Then it can take several weeks to determine the genetic characterization of the tumor. Depending upon the results and type of initial therapy chosen, a subsequent surgery may be necessary. If an accurate method to predict the genetics of the cancer through imaging (i.e., radiogenomics) alone could be developed, this would potentially minimize the number of surgeries and refine the type of therapy required.

The Radiological Society of North America (RSNA) has teamed up with the Medical Image Computing and Computer Assisted Intervention Society (the MICCAI Society) to improve diagnosis and treatment planning for patients with glioblastoma. In this competition you will predict the genetic subtype of glioblastoma using MRI (magnetic resonance imaging) scans to train and test your model to detect for the presence of MGMT promoter methylation.

If successful, you'll help brain cancer patients receive less invasive diagnoses and treatments. The introduction of new and customized treatment strategies before surgery has the potential to improve the management, survival, and prospects of patients with brain cancer.

## Dataset Description

The competition data is defined by three cohorts: Training, Validation (Public), and Testing (Private). The “Training” and the “Validation” cohorts are provided to the participants, whereas the “Testing” cohort is kept hidden at all times, during and after the competition.

These 3 cohorts are structured as follows: Each independent case has a dedicated folder identified by a five-digit number. Within each of these “case” folders, there are four sub-folders, each of them corresponding to each of the structural multi-parametric MRI (mpMRI) scans, in DICOM format. The exact mpMRI scans included are:

- Fluid Attenuated Inversion Recovery (FLAIR)
- T1-weighted pre-contrast (T1w)
- T1-weighted post-contrast (T1Gd)
- T2-weighted (T2)

## Open problems and opportunity areas
Areas where the literature suggests room for improvement. These are GAPS, not specific hypotheses — the Ideator generates hypotheses from these areas.

- **Vulnerability to Upstream Segmentation Errors**: Current mask fusion approaches assume perfect, ground-truth-like segmentation masks to crop the Region of Interest (ROI). The degradation in MGMT prediction when fed noisy, automated masks (or when propagating voxel-wise uncertainty from the segmentation model to the classification head) remains a critical gap in the literature. Joint training of segmentation and classification with uncertainty propagation is rarely explored due to memory constraints.
- **Susceptibility to Modality Redundancy and Noise**: Standard architectures assume all 4 MRI sequences (FLAIR, T1w, T1wCE, T2w) are beneficial. However, evidence suggests modality redundancy (e.g., T1w and T2w) can harm 3D CNN performance, highlighting a gap in dynamic, patient-specific modality weighting to automatically suppress uninformative or noisy sequences. This is particularly relevant for 3D models that are highly sensitive to the "curse of dimensionality".
- **Lack of Robustness to Missing Modalities**: Most benchmarks (like BraTS 2021) provide complete 4-sequence data, but clinical settings often have missing or corrupted scans. The literature lacks robust mechanisms to adapt mask fusion or foundation models when modalities are missing, leading to brittle pipelines. Developing fusion strategies that can gracefully degrade or impute missing spatial priors is a significant open challenge.
- **Integration of Explicit Geometric Radiomics with Deep Features**: While deep learning models excel at extracting abstract representations, they often ignore explicit geometric properties of the tumor (e.g., sphericity, surface area) that are known to correlate with MGMT status. Fusing these explicit geometric radiomic features with deep network outputs remains an underexplored area that could enhance tumor characterization and interpretability.
- **Lack of Explainability in Deep Radiogenomics**: While deep learning models achieve high performance, their 'black-box' nature hinders clinical adoption. The literature highlights a gap in integrating Explainable AI (XAI) techniques (e.g., generating voxel-wise uncertainty maps or attention heatmaps) directly into the training objective to build trust among neuroradiologists.

## What conventional approaches have already tried
- **Standard 3D CNNs and Vision Transformers** ([authors unknown — verify from source], 2024): Evaluated various deep learning architectures for predicting MGMT gene promoter methylation status from multi-parametric MRI. Achieved AUC 0.617 on BraTS 2021 using Xception, outperforming ViT3D (0.6006) and ResNet50 (0.5807).
  Limitation for our task: Struggles to isolate pathological features from healthy tissue noise, leading to poor predictive performance and severe overfitting on small datasets.
  Source: `[2024][IEEE CSDGAIS][DL-Radiogenomic]Deep_Learning_for_Brain_Tumor_Radiogenomic_Classif.md`
- **2D vs 3D ResNet Architectures** ([authors unknown — verify from source], 2024): Compared 2D and 3D ResNet architectures for predicting MGMT status. Achieved AUC 0.6168 with a 2D ResNet152 ensemble on the UCSF Glioma Dataset, while all 3D models failed to exceed an AUC of 0.5.
  Limitation for our task: 2D models suffer from slice-selection bias, missing volumetric context, while standard 3D models overfit severely without explicit spatial priors.
  Source: `[2024][arXiv][ResNet-Glioma]Comparative_Analysis_of_2D_and_3D_ResNet_Architect.md`
- **Brain Foundation Models with Hypergraph Dynamic Adapters** ([authors unknown — verify from source], 2025): Introduced SAM-Brain3D, a 3D brain-specific foundation model, combined with a Hypergraph Dynamic Adapter (HyDA) to model multi-modal relationships. Achieved a mean AUC of 0.644 on the BraTS 2021 MGMT classification task.
  Limitation for our task: General-purpose foundation models lack the explicit spatial priors needed to isolate the tumor microenvironment for radiogenomic classification.
  Source: `[2025][Pattern Recognition (PR)][SAM-Brain3D]Brain_Foundation_Models_with_Hypergraph_Dynamic_Ad.md`
- **Traditional Radiomics and Hybrid Feature Weighting** (Tasci et al., 2025): Utilized a hybrid feature weighting approach combining mRMR and LASSO with an SVM classifier. Achieved an AUC of 0.760 on the UPenn-GBM dataset.
  Limitation for our task: Handcrafted radiomics pipelines are difficult to optimize end-to-end and often fail to capture the complex, non-linear spatial hierarchies that deep learning models can learn when properly guided.
  Source: `[2024][Diagnostics][MGMT_ProFWise]Radiomics_and_AI_Based_Prediction_of_MGMT_Methylat.md`
- **Hybrid Deep Radiomics** ([authors unknown — verify from source], 2026): Proposed EnhancedMGMTNet, which fuses deep features from a 3D CNN encoder with handcrafted radiomics. Achieved an ROC-AUC of 0.871 on BraTS 2021.
  Limitation for our task: Fusing handcrafted features with deep features often leads to dimensionality mismatch and requires complex late-fusion strategies that are difficult to optimize end-to-end.
  Source: `[2026][arXiv][MGMT-XAI]Explainable_Deep_Radiogenomic_Molecular_Imaging_fo.md`
- **Domain Knowledge Augmented Mask Fusion** (Koska et al., 2025): Fused segmentation masks from T1W, T1CE, T2W, and FLAIR to create a single general pathology detection mask, which was then used to crop ROIs for a 3D CNN. Achieved an AUC of 0.900 on the BraTS 2021 dataset.
  Limitation for our task: The approach relies on perfect, pre-computed ground-truth segmentation masks and does not account for the uncertainty or potential failures of upstream automated segmentation models.
  Source: `[2025][Scientific Reports][MGMT-MaskFusion]Deep_learning_classification_of_MGMT_status_of_gli.md`
